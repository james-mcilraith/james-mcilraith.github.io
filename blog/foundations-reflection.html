Human blog

Foundations reflection

Friday, 27 September 2024

This human skills blog asks for Foundations reflection. About things learnt, the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning, and surprising things learnt during Foundations. It also touches on challenges and the importance of human skills.

  • What are three new things you have learnt about yourself and your ego due to the Human Skills learning?

What I’ve learnt about myself is mostly in relation to what’s new - what we have been learning about and how that has impacted me and influenced my ego.

  1. I’ve learned, or had it reinforced that the more I learn about software development, the more I realise that there is so much more to learn (or that I don’t know about). That learning realisation is always refreshingly humbling for the ego.
  2. I’ve learned that I am enjoying being critically engaged with new knowledge and skills, which is a rewarding place to be when starting out learning. My ego worries that the forthcoming bootcamp challenge, which is be even more fast-paced, may prove difficult for my more gradual learning style.
  3. I’ve learned, or had it reinforced that my natural chronological clock much prefers working a little later into the evening when my mind seems to be more focused. This is rather tragic for my ‘make the most of an early morning’ ego, as it means I end up going to bed later and often getting up a little later. That must, of course, change for bootcamp!
  • What are the role of values, empathy, and self-awareness in learning and programming?

Values, empathy, and self-awareness are interconnected and play an important part in how you approach learning and programming. And grow as a person and in relation to others.

Values speak to the principles that guide your thinking and actions. They reflect what is important to you. And can guide you in your day-to-day interactions and behaviours – as you learn and work together coding for example, supporting one another and building a body of human and programming knowledge and skills. Importantly, they also help guide those big picture conversations around what ‘we’ are doing and how ‘we’ are doing it. For instance, helping to answer, or at least provide some direction, on common ethical and privacy issues in software development, data collection and use, and in today’s context, around the employment and further development of AI.

Empathy is a particularly critical ingredient in developing connections and building relationships. Understanding and sharing the insights and feelings of others. And one that is helpful in any group or social situation, particularly where social connection and collaboration are important, and where there are everyday stresses and project or work-related anxieties. This may include in a programming learning environment, with lessons and assessments challenging folks, alongside providing learning opportunities and excitement.

Self-awareness is perhaps one of the most important factors in someone’s own journey – being able to look at and reflect upon your own thoughts and behaviours. Where your strengths and weaknesses reside and how you can improve upon your development and wellbeing. Self-awareness, or lack of, affects you and others. Without self-awareness you are less likely to be able to challenge yourself to change and improve. This in both a behavioural and practical sense – how you better work with others for example, or how you learn and apply yourself to coding projects.

  • What has surprised you the most about the Human Skills learning?

I’ve thoroughly enjoyed writing the blogs. I suppose this has surprised me a little coming from a social research background where I have been similarly engaged with human skills challenges and some of the topics. Perhaps it shouldn’t have surprised me! I think it is the blog writing itself and the format that I’ve enjoyed.

  • What were the most challenging aspects of the Human Skills learning?

I think, as some other may have experienced, that expressing your ideas – really reflecting on your own knowledge, skills, and person - is sometimes challenging. And doing so in a manner that is kind and respectful to yourself and the reader. Trying to reflect on things in a way that is also helpful to yourself and for the reader (where there were associated blog posts). I think the blogging itself is a useful challenging exercise i.e., as a journalling process.

  • Why do you think we, a programming school, are spending so much time focusing on Human Skills learning in a web development Bootcamp course?

Having been in the workplace for several years I support the importance of human skills. And that in most instances it is more important to hire someone who has good human skills (a good EQ) with great potential. Being able to work with others, get along well, and develop with the team are crucial. They make the difference.

  • Does the time you spent studying Human Skills learning here feel like a waste of time? Should you have just used that time to practice programming instead? Justify your answer.

I agree that human skills are vitally important. And don’t think it is a waste of time. In this training space, that learning just needs to be balanced appropriately with the technical ones. And that may be differing for different people given for example, peoples different backgrounds, experience, and learning requirements.
