Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Monday, 15 September 2024

This blog puts some thought into neuroplasticity and the growth mindset. And how that understanding might blend with my learning plan and strategies.

Resaerch neuroplasticity
  • Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people.

Neuroplasticity simply refers the brain's capacity to continue growing and evolving in response to life experiences. There are two main neuroplasticity types:

  • Functional neuroplasticity: This relates to the permanent structural changes in neural synapses. It's brought on by development and learning.
  • Structural neuroplasticity: This relates to the strength of the connections between neurons and changes to this strength.

Neuroplasticity can have a positive effect: It means that it is possible to change unhealthy behavior and thinking patterns – to develop new abilities, skills, memories, and mindsets. Importantly, it can also let you recover from traumatic brain injury.

  • Discuss how you might engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for your benefit.

There are most probably a few key neuroplasticity benefits that I would think particularly applicable:

  • It encourages learning and the needed learning effort.
  • It helps you think about what supports improved neuroplasticity e.g., diet, exercise, environment, meditation, and sleep.
  • It ultimately helps learning by increasing brain connections and memory.

This would be useful to apply during this learning period – knowing that the attitude and supports and the Dev Academy learning process contribute to and allow for improved (neuroplasticity) outcomes.

  • What are some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity?

When you look up the “principles of neuroplasticity” the top results suggest there are at several key principles. These are my favourites in terms of what contributes to people’s neuroplasticity:

  • Use it or lose it: You have to keep on using specific brain functions or it can lead to loss of abilities.
  • Use it and improve it: If you carry out training that drives a specific brain function it can lead to improving your abilities.
  • Specificity: The kind of the training experience determines the nature of the change in the brain (neuroplasticity).
  • Repetition matters: Change (neuroplasticity) requires enough repetition.
  • Intensity matters: Change (neuroplasticity) requires rigorous training.
  • Time matters: Different forms of change (neuroplasticity) in the brain happen at different times during training.
  • Salience matters: The training experience must be meaningful to the person for it to cause change (neuroplasticity).

My least favourite refers to age – that change from training occurs more easily in younger brains! Needless to say, all these points speak to the importance of deliberately engaging with learning and that with the effort comes positive change.

Resaerch growth mindset
  • Discuss what it is and why it is relevant.

Professor Carol Dweck talks about the growth mindset this way:

Individuals who believe their talents can be developed (through hard work, good strategies, and input from others) have a growth mindset. They tend to achieve more than those with a more fixed mindset (those who believe their talents are innate gifts)

- Dweck, 2016

Dweck also notes that everyone is actually a mixture of fixed and growth mindsets, and that mixture continually evolves with experience. Noting this what are some of the signs of a positive growth mindset (or characteristics you should encourage)?

  • Embrace challenges: See challenges as opportunities to learn and grow. You welcome the chance to stretch yourself and develop new skills.
  • Be open-minded: Be open to new ideas and viewpoints. You seek out feedback and constructive criticism, and you use it to improve and grow.
  • Be persistent: Put in the time and effort necessary to achieve your goals. You are not afraid of hard work and keep on going even when the going gets tough.
  • Embrace failure: Understand that failure is an inherent part of the learning process. You aren’t discouraged by setbacks. Instead, you view them as opportunities to learn, grow, and improve.
  • Recognise effort: Believe that effort and hard work together are crucial. You don't think that intelligence or natural talent alone are enough to achieve your goals.
  • Learn from others: Value the knowledge and expertise of others and are willing to learn from them. You are not afraid to ask questions and seek out guidance from others who can help you grow.
  • In this exploration, did anything surprise you? Change for you?

I think the thing that is crucial to recognise is that we are a mixture of both fixed and growth mindsets. And that we should strive to be more growth orientated, where possible. Also, that encouraging a growth mindset has to involve some quite practical steps – such as leaning into challenges and embracing failure as a learning opportunity. It is more than just having a positive outlook.

  • How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?

Hopefully, I am already some way there – leaning into the challenge of full stack development and the necessities that involves i.e., study and assessments etc. Having said that, the growth mindset also encourages the key learning steps outlined above e.g., challenges, open-mindedness, persistence etc.
